# Safe Companion — Weekly Scam News Digest

**Week of 2026-04-28 → 2026-05-04**

## What changed this week

The dominant theme in the last 7 days is *cash-out coercion* — scammers no longer settle for wires or gift cards, they're now walking victims to physical Bitcoin ATMs (FBI reports $333M lost in 2025, 80% from 60+) or shipping wrapped bills via FedEx after fake-IT-support pretexts. AARP escalated its anti-crypto-kiosk campaign in Boston/Nevada this week, and a tragic 70-hour "digital arrest" case in Hyderabad pushed that scam onto AARP's 2026 watch-list — it's now spreading from India into US/UK/CA. Two technical patterns also surged: fake-CAPTCHA "ClickFix" pages on mobile (auto-charging $30 in international SMS) and a sneaky abuse of *real* Apple/PayPal notification emails where scammers stuff phishing payloads into account-name fields. Safe Companion's existing payment warning, voice persona, and email scanner each cover *adjacent* ground but miss these specific gaps.

## Top 5 Feature Suggestions (ranked by impact × ease)

### 1. Crypto ATM Trip Detector
**Feature:** Crypto Kiosk Coercion Alarm
**Trigger story:** "Bitcoin ATMs are enabling scams, critics say. The AARP wants them gone." — Boston Globe, 2026-04-08; AARP / Nevada Current, 2026-04-03; FBI: $333M lost in 2025, 80%+ from age 60+
**Why it matters:** This is the single biggest "new" elder-fraud cash-out channel in 2026 — scammers (posing as Apple, Google, or "law enforcement") tell victims to drain their bank, drive to a 7-Eleven crypto kiosk, and feed in $100 bills one at a time using a QR wallet code the scammer texted. Safe Companion catches gift-card scripts and payment-app launches, but it has no concept of "user is being walked to a crypto ATM right now."
**Proposed solution:** Three-layer trip-blocker. (a) Maintain a small Room table of known crypto-kiosk operators (Bitcoin Depot, CoinFlip, Athena, RockItCoin) and detect their map searches via a Maps query intent listener / accessibility heuristic. (b) Watch for the specific QR-code-to-wallet pattern in the QR scanner (already ours) — if the destination is a crypto wallet address, full-screen red alert: *"Are you sending cash to a Bitcoin ATM because someone on the phone told you to? That is always a scam."* (c) Optional: detect Bitcoin-ATM SSIDs / GPS proximity and trigger a one-tap "I'm at a crypto kiosk" panic flow that texts family.
**AARP-friendliness (1–5):** 5 — full-screen plain-English warning at the moment of harm.
**Effort:** M
**Already covered?** Partially — gift-card alarm logic gives us scaffolding, but no crypto-kiosk awareness exists.

### 2. "Don't Mail Cash" Warning
**Feature:** Cash-Shipment Pretext Detector
**Trigger story:** "FBI issues new warning over tech support scams" — Tom's Guide, April 2026 (FBI: scammers now tell victims to wrap cash in a magazine and ship via FedEx/UPS to a name and address provided)
**Why it matters:** This is a brand-new payment vector specifically called out by the FBI in April. Once cash leaves in a parcel there's no chargeback, no bank reversal — it's worse than wire transfer. Safe Companion's payment-app warning never fires because no app is launched, and the gift-card alarm doesn't trigger. Pure social-engineering pretext.
**Proposed solution:** Add to scam-coaching content (Feature 53): a triggered card titled *"Were you told to wrap cash in a magazine and mail it?"* surfaced any time a chat/voicemail/SMS scan flags shipping language combined with cash, magazine, book, hidden, FedEx, UPS, USPS Priority. Add a home-screen quick-tile: "I was told to ship cash" → instant family alert + step-by-step *don't go to the post office* coaching. Also add 4 new Claude prompt patterns to the SLM classifier: "wrap the money," "conceal the cash," "ship via," "Priority Mail to."
**AARP-friendliness (1–5):** 5 — language is concrete, not technical.
**Effort:** S
**Already covered?** No — gift-card and wire-transfer alarms are the closest kin but don't fire on shipping pretexts.

### 3. Digital-Arrest Video-Call Coach
**Feature:** "Police Don't Video-Call You" Shield
**Trigger story:** "The Scam Where Fake Cops Put You Under 'Digital Arrest' Via Video Call" — ScamWatchHQ, 2026-04; AARP "Five Biggest Scams to Watch in 2026"; retired Hyderabad doctor died after 70 hours on a digital-arrest call
**Why it matters:** Spreading west from India where it was the #1 elder fraud in Q1. Scammers run real-time deepfake "police" video calls for hours, sometimes days, ordering the victim to stay on camera, not contact family, and transfer money to "secure their case." Safe Companion's voice agent and call-screening cover *audio* impostors well, but a long video call from WhatsApp or Zoom currently sails past us untouched.
**Proposed solution:** (a) Add a coaching card surfaced via the home screen and triggered by any voicemail/chat/email mentioning "warrant," "digital arrest," "remain on camera," "do not disconnect," or impersonated agency names (FBI, IRS, CBI, Interpol, your local PD). (b) When Safe Companion detects an active video-call app in the foreground (WhatsApp, Zoom, Skype, Telegram) for >5 minutes, show a subtle floating overlay: *"Are you on a video call with someone claiming to be police? Tap here."* — opens a 3-step confirm-with-family flow. (c) New persona one-tap line: "Grace, am I really under arrest?" plays the canned reassurance script with a Quick-Call-Family button.
**AARP-friendliness (1–5):** 5 — overlay only appears if the trigger words show up; otherwise invisible.
**Effort:** S
**Already covered?** No — call screening is voice-only and doesn't see WhatsApp/Zoom video calls at all.

### 4. Display-Name Phishing in Real Emails
**Feature:** Sender-Field Payload Scan
**Trigger story:** "Real Apple notifications are being used to drive tech support scams" — Malwarebytes, 2026-04
**Why it matters:** This is genuinely sneaky. Scammers create real Apple/PayPal/Microsoft accounts and stuff phishing text ("URGENT: call +1-855-…") into the *first-name / display-name / billing-address* fields. The victim then receives a *legitimate, signed, DKIM-passing* security alert from Apple — except the personal-info section is the lure. Safe Companion's Gmail scanner currently focuses on sender-domain reputation and link/URL analysis; if the email is from `noreply@apple.com` it gets a green tick. The phishing payload is in plain text inside the body and never goes through link analysis.
**Proposed solution:** Extend the email scanner to extract any user-controlled fields rendered in the email body (display name, "Hello, [name]" line, billing address line, account-change notification fields) and run them through the SLM scam classifier *separately* from the rest of the body. If they contain phone numbers, urgent verbs, or call-to-action language, flag SUSPICIOUS regardless of sender domain. Verdict copy: *"This email is real, but someone hid a fake message inside the account name. Do not call the number shown."*
**AARP-friendliness (1–5):** 5 — verdict explains the trick in one sentence.
**Effort:** S
**Already covered?** Partially — email scanning exists but trusts legit sender domains; this is a small surgical add.

### 5. Fake-CAPTCHA "ClickFix" Trap Warning
**Feature:** Suspicious-Page Paste Guard
**Trigger story:** "Fake CAPTCHA scam turns a quick click into a costly phone bill" — Malwarebytes, 2026-04; "Meet ClickFix" — Seraph Secure, 2026-03; The Hacker News reports 563% surge in CAPTCHA-based attacks in 2026
**Why it matters:** This is one of the fastest-growing mobile/desktop attack vectors of 2026. The page asks the user to "verify you're human" by pressing a key combo or pasting a command — on Android it triggers background international SMS sends (~$30 per victim); on PC it pastes a PowerShell command. Safe Companion's URL checker may green-light the host (these pages are often hosted on freshly-registered or compromised legitimate sites), and the Is-This-Safe screen has no "I'm being asked to paste something weird" path.
**Proposed solution:** (a) Add a new Is-This-Safe entry: *"A website asked me to verify or paste something."* It surfaces a 4-step photo-walkthrough: real CAPTCHAs never ask you to press Win+R or paste anything. (b) Detect SMS-bursts: if 3+ outgoing premium-rate / international SMS leave the device within 60 seconds without the user opening Messages, fire the panic alert. (c) Optional accessibility-service watcher for Chrome's address bar: if the active page contains the literal strings "Press Win+R" or "Press Cmd+V" or "I'm not a robot — Press" → floating yellow shield warning.
**AARP-friendliness (1–5):** 4 — needs the photo-walkthrough to be crystal clear; older users won't know what PowerShell is.
**Effort:** M
**Already covered?** No — URL checker and QR scanner don't observe in-page DOM content.
